Yellow-Rumped Warbler

The Yellow-Rumped Warbler (Setophaga Coronata) is a common species of bird that can be observed all across the North American continent.

These birds are concentrated in the continent’s northern parts during the breeding season and migrate towards southern North and Central America during winter. This species can generally be found in coniferous1 or mixed coniferous-decidious2 forests as these are their breeding habitats in summer. In winters they inhabit more open areas such as shrublands that offer food resources. In summer, both sexes are gray with a bit of white in the wings and yellow on the face, sides and rump3. Males have bright colors whereas females may show brown and generally have dull colors. Winter birds are paler brown, with a bright yellow rump and usually some yellow on the sides. These birds usually stay in the outer tree canopies4 at middle heights. They are active birds who often catch insects midair at middle heights and spend a lot of time eating berries from shrubs in winter. These birds like to travel in large flocks in winter.

There are two subspecies of the Yellow-rumped Warbler, ‘Audobon’ and ‘Myrtle’.

Audobon Myrtle
During spring, which is the migration and breeding season, Audobon’s Yellow-Rumped Warblers are blue-gray and with a bit of black above with white around their eyes and a yellow spot on their crowns. Myrtle Yellow-rumped Warblers are similar to Audobons in appearance with a few differences. Myrtles have white throats instead of yellow and males do not have white patched between their wing bars5.
They have yellow rumps and yellow spots on the side of their breasts which are sometimes covered by their wings. Audobon’s Yellow-rumped Warblers also have yellow throats. In breeding season, males have black masks with white lines above their eyes. They also have less black on their breasts.
In breeding season, females have two white wing bars and males have a significant amount of white between their wing bars. Males have brighter color than females and have blacker breasts and grayer backs. Adults in non-breeding season are duller, brown and gray. Immature birds are dull brown with a lack of yellow on their throats and heads. Juveniles look pretty much the same. The only difference is that Myrtles have more extensive pale throats than Audobons.

Additional Information

The Yellow-Rumped Warbler was found to be very flexible with its foraging methods. It can stand on a branch and catch bugs midair while clinging to a tree bark and it can also skim water surfaces.

The Yellow-rumped Warbler builds its nest anywhere from 4 to 50 feet off the ground usually in the high branches of a pine tree. Females construct this cup-like structure in approximately 10 days and occasionally use the materials offered by the males. The nests use feathers and animal hair which are curved upwards over the cup-shaped nest to partially conceal the little eggs. Their diet solely consists of insects in the summer and they spend winters in tropical locations. The Yellow-Rumped Warbler can migrate further than other warblers in winter because of its ability to digest the wax in bayberries and wax myrtles. This Warbler also eats berries of poison oak, juniper and Virginia creeper along with seeds from goldenrod and beach grasses.

They are found throughout the United States at some point in the year. Males usually forage higher in the trees than females do.

The Yellow-rumped Warbler is one of the first birds to start eating berries in Autumn as it can digest bayberries and way myrtle berries. These Warblers get their food by picking insects from seaweed6, skimming the top of water for insects, raiding spider webs for a bite and even hunting through piles of manure.

Interesting facts about warblers

  1. The Yellow-Rumped Warbler is one of the most common Warblers in North America and is capable of migrating farther north than any other Warbler in winter.
  2. It has the ability to digest 80% of wax-coated berries such as bayberries.
  3. The 2 types of subspecies, “Audobon” and “Myrtle”, were once considered separate species. Due to recent generic testing, the same might occur again and these two might be treated as two different species in the future.
  4. A group of warblers has many collective nouns, including a "bouquet", "confusion", "fall", and "wrench" of warblers.

Threats to Warblers

The Yellow-Rumped Warbler is a common bird and is nowhere near being endangered as it is widespread, and its numbers are increasing. However, this species, like many other common birds, suffers from human inventions and technology that we built.

  • These birds get caught up in accidents such as collision with buildings, glass, towers and man-made structures. Adding to that, human transportation also contributes to the increase in death rates of these birds as they have less space to roam around.
  • Communication towers also contribute a lot to the death rates of these birds. A 2013 studyshowed that, in the regions tracked, Yellow-rumped Warblers were among the top 10 species killed at communication towers.

Definitions

1) Coniferous:
Coniferous trees bear cones and have needles or scales that do not fall off.
2) Decidous:
Deciduous trees have leaves that fall off yearly
3) Rump:
A bird's rump is the patch above the tail and low on the back
4) Canopy:
Tree canopy means the layer of leaves, branches, and stems of trees that cover the ground when viewed from above
5) Wing Bars:
A line of contrasting color across the middle of a bird's wing made by markings on the wing coverts.
6) Seaweed:
"Seaweed" is the common name for countless species of marine plants and algae that grow in the ocean as well as in rivers, lakes, and other water bodies.
